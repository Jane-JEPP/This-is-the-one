Features of Early–Mid 20th Century Photography

The 20th century reshaped photography more dramatically than any era before it. Advances in technology—portable cameras, faster film, color processes, and later digital sensors—freed photographers from rigid studio setups.

This century produced some of the most influential visual voices in history, including Dorothea Lange, Robert Capa, and Vivian Maier.

Dorothea Lange (Active 1920s–1960s)

Best known for documenting the Great Depression, Lange used her camera to reveal the human cost of economic collapse.

    Key elements of Lange’s influence:
  • Humanized national suffering through honest portraiture
  • Worked for federal agencies documenting rural hardship
  • Produced some of the most iconic documentary images ever captured

Robert Capa (Active 1930s–1950s)

A pioneering war photographer, Robert Capa reshaped conflict journalism through courage and immediacy.

    Capa’s Legacy:
  1. Revolutionized war photography
  2. Co-founded Magnum Photos
  3. Set new standards for:
    • Proximity to danger
    • Authenticity of storytelling
    • Human-centered images from the front lines

Vivian Maier (Active 1950s–1970s)

Vivian Maier, a previously unknown nanny who photographed privately, became one of the most important street photographers of the 20th century after her work was discovered posthumously.

Her style helped define modern street photography—intimate yet observational.

Maier spent much of her life photographing the streets of Chicago — which is why the “Play Game” button above leads to a small game set in Chicago as a fun tribute.

    Maier’s Impact:
  • Elevated unnoticed everyday moments
  • Explored themes of anonymity and authenticity
  • Inspired renewed interest in candid street photography
